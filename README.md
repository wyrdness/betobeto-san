## Overview

Betobeto-san (べとべとさん) is a Japanese yōkai of the auditory kind — known not by appearance but by the sound of footsteps following a traveller on a quiet night road. The name imitates the 'beto-beto' sound of sandals slapping the ground. The creature is invisible, never harms anyone, and is reported chiefly from Uda-gun in Nara Prefecture and from Shizuoka Prefecture, with related auditory yōkai (Bishakutsu) recorded in Fukui Prefecture. The folkloric remedy is to stop, step aside on the road, and politely say something like 'Betobeto-san, please go ahead' (Betobeto-san, osaki ni okoshi); the footsteps then continue on without you. Betobeto-san is one of the best-known minor yōkai in Japanese folklore compendiums and is most familiar to modern audiences through manga artist Shigeru Mizuki, who included it in his GeGeGe no Kitarō works.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: Japanese onomatopoeia: 'beto-beto' (べとべと) imitating the sound of footsteps, with the honorific suffix -san. The yōkai is identified by sound rather than form.
- **Meaning**: Roughly 'Mr. Sticky-Steps' or 'Mr. Patter-Patter' — named for its distinctive footstep sound.
- **First use**: Recorded in 20th-century Japanese folklore compendiums, including the Sōgō Nihon Minzoku Goi (Comprehensive Vocabulary of Japanese Folk Customs, 1955) and the Yōkai Jiten (Yōkai Encyclopedia) tradition.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Yōkai of the road** _(Yanagita Kunio (folklore tradition), Shigeru Mizuki)_

Treated as one of a class of harmless but eerie auditory yōkai accompanying solitary walkers at night.

### Scientific

**Auditory pareidolia and lone-walker psychology** _(Folklorists (e.g. Komatsu Kazuhiko))_

The phenomenon plausibly reflects ordinary auditory pareidolia: the human tendency to perceive familiar patterns (footsteps) in random ambient noise on a quiet road, especially when alone and apprehensive.

### Skeptical

**Folkloric explanation only** _(Mainstream Japanese folklore studies)_

Betobeto-san is universally treated as folklore; there is no claim that it is a flesh-and-blood entity to be debunked.
